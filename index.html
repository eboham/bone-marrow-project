About the Project

Background

Plasmodium falciparum has a complex life cycle, in which asexual replication and sexual development take place in red blood cells (RBCs) of the human host and sexual reproduction in the mosquito vector. Though the asexual stages are responsible for all morbidity and mortality, successful transmission is dependent on generation of the sexual stages, termed gametocytes.

The human bone marrow (hBM) is a major site for the sequestration of P. falciparum gametocytes and acts as a privileged niche for their maturation and protection. Recent studies specifically revealed that P. falciparum gametocytes are readily and preferentially found in the hBM extravascular spaces, in contact with several stromal cell types.

Research Objectives

Task 1: Histological Analysis

Analyzing human bone marrow biopsies from malaria-infected asymptomatic individuals to identify P. falciparum gametocytes' presence and localization within the bone marrow microenvironment.

Task 2: Co-culture Systems

Establishing 3D co-culture systems using bone marrow stromal and endothelial cells along with P. falciparum-infected cells to examine how bone marrow host cells influence gametocytogenesis induction.

Task 3: Epidemiological Study

Collecting molecular and histological data from malaria-infected individuals during two transmission seasons to investigate epidemiological parameters associated with P. falciparum gametocyte presence in bone marrow.

Study Location

Malaria is endemic in all parts of Ghana including the Volta and Northern regions, where transmission is seasonal. The average asymptomatic malaria prevalence ranges from 35-66%. The study sites have an average of 3-14 orthopedic surgeries per week yielding bone marrow/biopsy donations with 40% malaria parasite positivity by PCR.

Expected Impact

This research project's multi-faceted approach contributes to a comprehensive understanding of the complex interplay between the parasite and its host, shedding light on potential factors affecting gametocytogenesis and transmission dynamics. The findings will inform new strategies for malaria control and elimination efforts.

The collaboration between the ISS and Dr. Dinko's team dates back to the ISS workshop "Malaria Transmission - Cellular Biology and Human Genetics" in 2018. The partnership was strengthened through the UK Royal Society collaborative grant "Origin and development of maturing gametocytes of Plasmodium falciparum in deep tissues and peripheral circulation" and continues to develop through the Italia-Africa PhD program.

Timeline of Achievements

June 2024 - Project Initiation

PhD candidate Eric Boham arrived in Rome and began comprehensive training in malaria parasite culture, histological techniques, and immunohistochemistry assays.

July-August 2024 - Sample Processing

25 bone marrow biopsies from Ghanaian asymptomatic malaria patients were processed and embedded in paraffin. Hematoxylin and eosin staining completed for all samples.

September 2024 - Technical Optimization

Identified technical challenges with sample fragmentation. Adopted Hammersmith protocol for improved sample processing. Participated in BeMM Symposium at Sapienza University.

October 2024 - Immunohistochemistry

Began immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry assays using anti-Pfg 27 and anti-Pfs 16 antibodies for gametocyte identification. Image acquisition with 3D-Thunder microscope initiated.

2025 - Upcoming Milestones

Technology transfer to KNUST, establishment of co-culture systems, and collection of new samples during malaria transmission seasons in Ghana.
